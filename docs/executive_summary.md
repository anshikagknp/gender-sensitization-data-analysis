# Executive Summary

This summary covers *Perspectives on Gender Sensitization: A Kanpur-based Comprehensive Study*, an MA Political Science dissertation that examines how gender-sensitized the youth of Kanpur city are, based on a 100-respondent survey.

## Background

Gender sensitization is the process of helping people recognize and challenge unfair gender-based assumptions, stereotypes, and behaviours, with the goal of promoting equal and respectful treatment across genders. This study set out to gauge how sensitized Kanpur's youth currently are, where the gaps in awareness and training lie, and what could close them.

## Objectives

- Explore how perspectives on gender sensitization vary across age, gender, and educational background.
- Assess the current level of awareness and understanding of gender sensitization among Kanpur's youth.
- Generate actionable recommendations for improving gender sensitization training in schools and workplaces.

Two hypotheses were tested: that Kanpur youth (18–35) are fairly gender sensitized, and that schools and workplaces lack adequate gender-sensitization training.

## Methodology Snapshot

A quantitative, closed-ended questionnaire was distributed via Google Forms using convenience and stratified sampling, gathering responses from 100 people — 96% of them aged 18–35, and a near-even 53/47 male-female split. Full detail in `methodology.md`.

## Key Findings

- **Awareness outpaces understanding:** 77% had heard the term "gender sensitization," but only ~39% correctly identified gender as non-binary/socially constructed — most respondents recognize the term without a firm grasp of the underlying concept.
- **Training gap:** 60% had never received any gender-related education or training.
- **Stereotypes:** Roughly two-thirds of respondents rejected common gender stereotypes on average (e.g., 82% said it's okay for men to cry; 75% rejected "men are strong, women are weak"), though belief in some — like "men are rational, women are emotional" (33% agreement) — remains notable.
- **Roles:** About 78% rejected rigid, predefined domestic gender roles on average.
- **Equality:** About 74% supported gender-equality measures on average, including 90.8% backing equal pay for equal work.
- **Violence:** About 70% opposed gender-based violence and any tolerance of it, on average.
- **Appetite for training:** 81% support adding gender sensitization to school curricula; 74% support workplace training programs.

Full breakdown in `key_findings.md`.

## Conclusion

On balance, around 70% of Kanpur's young population can be considered fairly gender-sensitized, which the dissertation reads as support for its first hypothesis. But persistent gaps in formal training and conceptual clarity (sex vs. gender) support its second hypothesis — that structured sensitization programs in schools and workplaces remain inadequate. The study recommends wider curriculum-based and workplace gender-sensitization programs, alongside better policy implementation and feedback mechanisms.

*Scope note: the study did not include the perspectives of LGBTQIA+ communities, due to limited respondent availability.*
