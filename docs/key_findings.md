# Key Findings

Results below are drawn from the dissertation's Chapter 4 (100-respondent Google Forms survey). Response counts (*n*) vary slightly by question — from 93 to 100 — because not every respondent answered every item.

## 1. Gender Sensitization Awareness

| Question | Result | n |
|---|---|---|
| Heard the term "Gender Sensitisation" before? | Yes 77% · No 23% | 100 |
| Is gender binary or non-binary? | Non-binary 39.4% · Binary 38.4% · Unsure 22.2% | 99 |
| Ever received gender-related training/education? | Yes 39.8% · No 60.2% | 93 |
| Should gender sensitisation be in school curricula? | Yes, definitely 81.4% · Maybe 10.3% · No, not required 8.3% | 97 |
| Should gender sensitisation be in workplace training? | Yes, definitely 74% · Maybe 15% · No, not required 11% | 100 |
| Important for healthy relationships? | Yes 75% · Maybe 17.7% · No 7.3% | 96 |

**Takeaway:** Familiarity with the term outpaces conceptual understanding — most respondents recognize "gender sensitization," but a majority cannot confidently identify gender as non-binary, and most have never been formally trained. Support for institutional training, in both schools and workplaces, is strong.

## 2. Gender Stereotypes

| Statement | Agree | Disagree | Neutral | n |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Men are rational, women are emotional | 33.3% | 43.4% | 23.2% | 99 |
| Women are generally bad drivers | 13% | 66% | 21% | 100 |
| It is okay for men to cry | 82% | 13% | 5% | 100 |
| Men are strong, women are weak | 16% | 75% | 9% | 100 |
| Women are better housekeepers, men better with machines | 21% | 62% | 17% | 100 |
| Being gentle makes men girlish/less masculine | 23.2% | 67.7% | 9.1% | 99 |

**Takeaway:** Most overt stereotypes are rejected by a majority, but belief is far from negligible — roughly a third still endorse the "rational men / emotional women" framing, and around a fifth to a quarter still hold stereotypes tied to masculinity, competence, and domestic roles.

## 3. Gender Roles

| Question | Result | n |
|---|---|---|
| Are diapering/bathing/feeding kids only the mother's job? | Yes 23.7% · No 76.3% | 97 |
| Is a woman's role to keep house while the man provides? | Yes 34.7% · No 65.3% | 98 |
| Should chores be split equally if both partners work? | Yes, definitely 93.9% · No, woman should manage both 6.1% | 98 |

**Takeaway:** A strong majority reject rigid caregiving and breadwinning roles, especially for dual-income households — though about a third still primarily associate homemaking with women.

## 4. Gender Equality

| Question | Result | n |
|---|---|---|
| Equal pay for equal work? | Yes 90.8% · No 9.2% | 98 |
| Favour systems guaranteeing gender diversity (govt./education/executive roles)? | Yes 70.4% · No 13.3% · Unsure 16.3% | 98 |
| Does gender-based discrimination happen around you? | Yes 61.2% · No 24.5% · Unsure 14.3% | 98 |
| Favour gender-neutral language? | Yes 69.7% · No 9.1% · Maybe 21.2% | 99 |
| Should girls pursue careers traditionally associated with men? | Yes 84.8% · No 5.1% · Maybe 10.1% | 99 |
| Should a man have final say on home decisions? | Yes 18.2% · No 66.7% · Maybe 15.2% | 99 |

**Takeaway:** Support is highest for economic fairness (equal pay) and encouraging girls into traditionally male careers, and somewhat lower — though still majority — for diversity quotas and shared decision-making authority. A majority (61.2%) say they personally witness gender-based discrimination.

## 5. Gender-Based Violence

| Question | Result | n |
|---|---|---|
| Should a woman tolerate violence to keep her family together? | No 70.4% · Yes 24.5% · Maybe 5.1% | 98 |
| Is it okay to make the opposite gender uncomfortable with sexist jokes? | No 76.8% · Yes 17.2% · Maybe 6% | 99 |
| Can gender sensitisation programs reduce gender-based violence? | Yes 63.3% · No 14.3% · Maybe 22.4% | 98 |

**Takeaway:** A strong majority reject normalizing violence and sexist behaviour, and most believe sensitization programs help — though nearly a quarter of respondents condone or are ambivalent about a woman tolerating violence "for the family," a notable pocket of resistance.

## Overall Conclusions (as stated in the dissertation)

- About **60%** of respondents could not clearly distinguish *sex* from *gender*.
- About **60%** had never received any gender-related training.
- On average, about **65%** reject predefined gender stereotypes.
- On average, about **78%** reject predefined gender roles — though the dissertation notes a possible gap between stated attitudes and actual behaviour.
- On average, about **74%** favour gender equality across the areas surveyed.
- On average, about **70%** oppose gender-based violence across the scenarios tested.
- **Overall, ~70% of Kanpur's youth are assessed as fairly gender-sensitized** — with clear room for improvement in formal training and conceptual understanding.

## Recommendations

1. Broaden gender-sensitization curricula in schools (currently limited to a handful of institutions, e.g. Kendriya Vidyalaya schools).
2. Introduce structured gender-sensitization training in workplaces.
3. Strengthen implementation and feedback loops for existing government/institutional programs (e.g., those run by the National Commission for Women).
4. Close the gap between *awareness of the term* "gender sensitization" and deeper conceptual understanding (sex vs. gender, non-binary identities).

*Derived from: Perspectives on Gender Sensitization: A Kanpur-based Comprehensive Study (Anshika Gupta, Christ Church College, Kanpur, 2023–24).*
